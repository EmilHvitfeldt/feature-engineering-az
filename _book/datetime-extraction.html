41  Value Extraction

Sometimes date variables come in many data sets and problems. The presence of a date time variable doesnâ€™t mean that we are working with a time series data set. If you are, then read more about how to handle that in Chapter 114. All the extract methods are not learned, as there isnâ€™t anything to estimate.

Dates and date times are typically stores as integers. With dates represented as the number of days since 1970-01-01, with negative values for earlier dates. with date times representing the number of seconds since 1970-01-01.

This means that if your are lucky and the model you were trying to fit knew to convert dates and date times into integers, and that â€œtime since 1970-01-01â€ would be a helpful predictor. If not then we need to do some more advanced work.

We have assumed for this chapter that we already have the date variables in the right format. This would typically be

YYYYâ€MMâ€DD

for dates, and

YYYYâ€MMâ€DD HH:MM:SS

for date times.

Note

There are other format, and it isnâ€™t too big of a deal, as long as the libraries you are using can handle them.

There is also the wrinkle with regards to time zones, leap years and leap seconds. There are many rules that can miss you up. It is for this reason that we recommend that you use a trusted date time library to do the following calculations.

Most libraries allows us to pull out standard measurements like

  • year
  • month
  • day
  • hour
  • minute
  • second

but we can include a couple more. quarter, semester, week. These are highly related to the above list, they are just on a different format. season is another possibly nice feature, but we have to be careful as the seasons flip depending on where on the globe we are at.

In our above list of features, each of them counts up from 1, until we reach the level level. So minutes stops at 60 and starts over. This might not be what we want, so we can include finer detail by extracting seconds in day, days in week, and days in year. If will be up to you to figure out if these are useful for you. Generally these become useful in the broader sense. seconds in day is more finely grained than hour, so if you want to figure out time of day, then the smaller measurements are helpful. Likewise, some of these methods are better expressed as decimals, as periods such as month have different lengths, and will thus be different. Talking about being 0.9 through the month will be more precise.

From these we can also do things like weekends, weekdays, morning and holidays such as Christmas and Easter. Remember that there are libraries to extract these for you.

Some of the measurements here can be extracted as categorical rather than numeric, things like day of week can either be extracted as 1, 2, 3 or Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday or month that can be extracted . We can think of the numeric extract as being a automatic label encoding Chapter 21 of the categorical version. Some times it will be worth extracting the categorical version and use it directly or embed it with other methods in Chapter 17. It is worth noting that the categorical version of these variables are ordinal in nature.

Note

Most of the advise here tracks to sub-second measurements too if that is applicable to your problem.

The talk in this chapter is very Euro and US focused. Many cultures around the world divide the â€œyearâ€ and â€œdayâ€ up differently. Always use the conventions that are most appropriate with the culture you are working with.

41.1 Pros and Cons

41.1.1 Pros

  • Fast and easy computations
  • Can provide good results

41.1.2 Cons

  • The numerical features generated are all increasing with time linearly
  • There are a lot of extractions, and they correlate quite a bit
